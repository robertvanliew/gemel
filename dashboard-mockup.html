The five numbers to check before any spread

1
Max loss in dollars

Spread width minus credit, × 100. If this number is more than 2% of the account, the trade is too big — full stop, before any other analysis.

2
Short-leg delta

Roughly the odds the short strike finishes in the money. The 0.15–0.25 band trades small, frequent wins against occasional defined losses.

3
IV rank

Credit spreads sell volatility. Below ~25, the credit collected is thin for the risk taken — pass and wait.

4
Break-even

Short strike minus credit. Ask: does price have room and support above this line?

5
The calendar

Earnings, FOMC, CPI before expiry? One scheduled event can move price through both strikes overnight.

Glossary — every term used anywhere in this app

Contract basics

Option contract
The right, not the obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) 100 shares at a set price by a set date. The buyer pays for that right; the seller is paid to take on the matching obligation. Credit spreads put you on the seller side, with protection.
Call
An option that gains value as the stock rises. Buying one is a bullish bet; selling one obligates you to deliver shares at the strike if exercised.
Put
An option that gains value as the stock falls. Selling a put — the engine of this whole playbook — means being paid to promise to buy shares at the strike.
Strike price
The fixed price the contract trades shares at if exercised. A spread always involves two strikes: one sold, one bought.
Premium
What an option costs. Quoted per share, so a $1.42 premium is $142 per contract.
Expiration
The date the contract stops existing. After it, options are either exercised (in the money) or expire worthless (out of the money).
Contract multiplier
Every standard option controls 100 shares, so all per-share numbers get multiplied by 100. A $5-wide spread risks up to $500 per contract, not $5.
Intrinsic value
What an option would be worth if exercised right now. A $575 put with the stock at $570 has $5 of intrinsic value; with the stock at $581, it has none.
Extrinsic value
Everything in the premium beyond intrinsic value — payment for time and uncertainty. This is what a credit-spread seller collects and what theta erodes away.
ITM / ATM / OTM
In, at, or out of the money. A put is ITM when the stock is below its strike, ATM right at it, OTM above it. Spreads here are opened OTM and the goal is for them to stay that way.
Exercise
The option buyer using their right to trade shares at the strike. As a seller you don't exercise — you can only be assigned.
Assignment
Being on the receiving end of exercise. If your short put finishes ITM, you buy 100 shares at the strike. The long leg of a spread offsets this; a cash-secured put leaves you owning the stock.
Early assignment
Assignment before expiration. Rare on OTM spreads, more likely when a short option is deep ITM with little extrinsic value left — one reason positions get closed by 21 DTE instead of held to the end.
Pin risk
The stock closing almost exactly at your short strike on expiration day, leaving you unsure whether you'll be assigned overnight. Avoided entirely by closing before expiration.
Ex-dividend risk
Short calls can be assigned early just before a dividend, since the option holder wants the payout. Matters once bear call spreads or iron condors are in play.

Spread construction

Vertical spread
Two options of the same type and expiration at different strikes, one sold and one bought. The family name for bull put and bear call spreads.
Credit spread
A vertical where the option you sell is worth more than the one you buy, so cash lands in the account up front. The bought leg caps the maximum loss — defined risk by construction.
Debit spread
The mirror image: you pay to open because the bought leg costs more than the sold one. A directional bet rather than an income trade — not part of this playbook yet.
Bull put spread
Sell a put, buy a cheaper put below it. Profits if the stock rises, drifts sideways, or even falls a little — it only loses if price drops through the short strike by more than the credit.
Bear call spread
Sell a call, buy a cheaper call above it. The bearish mirror image of a bull put spread, used when the regime gate flips down.
Iron condor
A bull put spread and a bear call spread on the same ticker at once. Collects two credits and profits if price stays inside the range. Defined risk on both sides, but two short strikes to defend.
Cash-secured put
A short put with the full strike value held in cash, no protective leg. The credit is larger but risk is undefined down to zero — which is why it can blow through a 2% per-trade cap.
Spread width
The distance between the two strikes. Width minus credit is the maximum loss: a $5-wide spread that collects $1.42 risks $3.58 per share, $358 per contract.
Legs / legging in
Each option in a spread is a leg. Entering both legs as one order gets one fill at one price; legging in (placing them separately) risks the market moving between fills.
Defined vs undefined risk
Defined risk means the worst case is known and capped before entry (spreads, condors). Undefined risk means losses can keep growing (naked puts, CSPs to zero). The dashboard's guardrails exist to flag the difference.

The Greeks

Delta (Δ)
Two jobs: how much the option price moves per $1 move in the stock, and a rough probability the option expires in the money. A 0.18-delta short put ≈ an 18% chance of being tested.
Gamma
How fast delta itself changes as the stock moves. Gamma explodes near expiration, which makes the final weeks twitchy for short options — the reason behind the 21-DTE exit.
Theta
Time decay, the income source of a credit spread. Every day price stays above the short strike, the spread gets cheaper to buy back. Theta works for the seller and against the buyer.
Vega
Sensitivity to changes in implied volatility. A short spread profits when IV falls after entry and hurts when it spikes — which is why selling when IV rank is already high stacks the odds.
Rho
Sensitivity to interest rates. Real, but negligible at 30–45 DTE — the one Greek safely ignored at this stage.

Volatility

Implied volatility (IV)
The market's forecast of how much the stock will move, baked into option prices. High IV = expensive options = fatter credits for sellers.
Historical volatility
How much the stock actually moved in the past. Comparing it to IV shows whether options are pricing in more drama than the stock typically delivers.
IV rank
Where today's IV sits versus its own past year, 0–100. An IVR of 34 means IV is higher than it's been 34% of the time. Below ~25, credits are too thin for the risk — the scanner's volatility gate.
IV percentile
A cousin of IV rank: the percentage of days in the past year IV closed lower than today. Same idea, slightly different math — know which one a platform is quoting.
Expected move
The price range the options market implies for a period, roughly one standard deviation. Useful sanity check: is the short strike outside the expected move?
Volatility crush
A sharp IV collapse after a known event (earnings, FOMC) passes. Great for positions opened before it at high IV — brutal for options bought into it.
Skew
OTM puts usually carry higher IV than OTM calls because markets fear crashes more than rallies. It's why put credit spreads tend to pay better than the call side.

Trade math

Credit
The cash collected up front when the spread is sold. It's also the maximum profit — the best case is keeping all of it.
Max profit
The full credit, earned if both legs expire worthless. In practice the playbook takes 50% early rather than waiting for the last dollar.
Max loss
Spread width minus credit, times 100. Known before entry and the first of the five numbers checked — if it exceeds 2% of the account, nothing else matters.
Break-even
Short strike minus credit (for put spreads). At expiration, every cent above it is profit territory; below it, losses grow until the long leg caps them.
Return on risk
Credit divided by max loss. The $1.42 credit against $3.58 of risk is ≈40% — the yardstick for comparing candidate spreads.
Credit-to-width ratio
Credit divided by spread width. Collecting roughly a third of the width is a common benchmark for a fairly-priced spread at these deltas.
Probability of profit (POP)
The modeled odds the trade makes at least $0.01. Closely tied to the short-leg delta — a 0.18-delta spread has roughly an 80%+ POP, paired with smaller wins than losses.
Delta band
The 0.15–0.25 short-leg range this playbook sells in. Tighter than 0.15 pays too little; richer than 0.25 gets tested too often.

The technical screen

EMA (20 / 50)
Exponential moving average — average price with extra weight on recent days. Price above the 20- and 50-day EMAs is this system's definition of a healthy uptrend; the 50-EMA is the master gate.
SMA
Simple moving average, every day weighted equally. Slower to react than an EMA; some traders use the 200-day SMA as the big-picture trend line.
RSI
Relative Strength Index, 0–100 — a momentum gauge of recent gains versus losses. The screen wants 40–70: enough strength to confirm the trend, not so much that the move is stretched.
Overbought / oversold
RSI above ~70 or below ~30. Overbought doesn't mean 'about to fall' — it means the easy part of the move may be done, a poor moment to sell a put spread underneath it.
ADX
Average Directional Index — trend strength, regardless of direction. Low ADX means a drifting, range-bound market: the regime where iron condors eventually take over from directional spreads.
Support / resistance
Price zones where buyers (support) or sellers (resistance) have repeatedly stepped in. The screen wants clear support between current price and the short strike — a floor before the trade is wrong.
Trend regime
The market's current personality: trending up, trending down, or choppy. Strategies aren't good or bad in a vacuum — they're matched or mismatched to regime, which is what the playbook table enforces.
Event risk
Scheduled news with the power to jump price through both strikes overnight — earnings, FOMC meetings, CPI prints. The screen disqualifies any ticker with one before expiration.

Execution & account

Bid-ask spread
The gap between what buyers offer and sellers ask. Wide gaps are a hidden tax on every entry and exit — liquid ETFs like SPY keep it to pennies.
Mid price
Halfway between bid and ask, the usual starting point for a limit order. Getting filled at or near mid on both legs is part of the edge.
Slippage
The difference between the price expected and the price actually filled. Small per trade, meaningful across a hundred trades — the backtest models it for that reason.
Open interest
How many contracts exist at a strike. Healthy open interest means real two-sided markets and easier exits.
Volume
Contracts traded today. Volume plus open interest is the quick liquidity check before trusting any quoted price.
Buying power reduction
Capital the broker sets aside while a position is open. A defined-risk spread reserves roughly its max loss; a cash-secured put reserves the entire strike value — the dashboard's BP card makes that visible.
Margin
Borrowed capacity from the broker. This playbook never uses it: every position is either defined-risk or fully cash-secured.
Fill
The actual execution of an order. 'Working' an order means starting at mid and adjusting a cent at a time rather than crossing the whole spread instantly.

Process & performance

Win rate
Percentage of trades closed profitably. High-probability spreads naturally win often — which is exactly why win rate alone is misleading without the next metric.
Profit factor
Gross wins ÷ gross losses. Above 1 the system makes money; below 1 it doesn't, no matter how pretty the win rate. The single most honest summary number on the Analytics page.
Equity curve
Cumulative P&L over time, plotted. The shape matters as much as the endpoint — steady stairs beat a spike followed by a cliff.
Max drawdown
The deepest peak-to-trough drop the account suffered. The number that decides whether a strategy is emotionally survivable, not just mathematically positive.
Rule adherence
Percentage of trades where every checklist rule was followed. It separates 'the strategy didn't work' from 'I didn't follow the strategy' — and it gates position sizing.
Paper trading
Simulated trades with fake money and real discipline. The first six weeks live here; the journal treats paper and real trades identically.
Position sizing
How much risk one trade carries. The 2% cap means even a string of full losses dents the account instead of ending it.
Time stop
A calendar-based exit — close or roll at 21 DTE regardless of P&L, before gamma and assignment risk pick up.
Rolling
Closing a spread and reopening at a later expiration, often new strikes, to give the thesis more time. One idea across multiple tickets — journaled as a single campaign.
Campaign
A chain of rolled positions judged as one trade. Prevents a roll's closing leg from polluting the stats as a standalone loss.
In-sample / out-of-sample
In-sample is the history a strategy was tuned on; out-of-sample is reserved history it never saw. Performance should degrade only modestly out-of-sample.
Overfitting
A strategy sculpted so tightly to past data that it memorized noise instead of learning signal. Big in-sample vs out-of-sample gaps are the tell.
